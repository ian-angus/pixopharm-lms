## 1. PRIMARY LEGISLATION GOVERNING PHARMACY PRACTICE IN JAMAICA

Jamaica's pharmacy practice is governed by a suite of interconnected legislation:

| Legislation | Year | Primary Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **The Pharmacy Act** | 1966 (Act 5 of 1966) | Registration of pharmacists, pharmacies, pharmacy owners; regulation of compounding, dispensing, storing, and retailing of drugs and poisons |
| **The Food and Drugs Act** | 1964 | Regulation of foods, drugs, cosmetics, and medical devices; drug classification and scheduling |
| **Food and Drugs Regulations** | 1975 | Implementing regulations under s.21 of the Food and Drugs Act; detailed drug lists and dispensing requirements |
| **The Dangerous Drugs Act** | 1948 | Monitoring and control of narcotic drugs (cocaine, morphine) and psychotropic substances (ganja/cannabis) |
| **Dangerous Drugs (Amendment) Act** | 2015 ("Ganja Law") | Decriminalization of small cannabis possession; establishment of Cannabis Licensing Authority; Rastafarian religious use |
| **The Precursor Chemicals Act** | 1999 (operational Dec 3, 2013) | Monitoring and control of precursor chemicals used in illicit drug manufacturing |
| **The Data Protection Act** | 2020 (effective Dec 1, 2020) | Protection of personal data including health information |
| **National Health Fund Act** | 2003 | Establishment of contributory health insurance system; pharmaceutical procurement and distribution |

### 1.1 The Pharmacy Act, 1966 (Act 5 of 1966)

**Purpose:** To repeal the Drugs and Poisons law, provide for the registration of Pharmacists, Pharmaceutical students, pharmacies and owners of pharmacies, and to regulate the compounding, dispensing, storing for sale and retailing of drugs and poisons.

**Structure (Arrangement of Sections):**

| Part | Subject Matter |
|---|---|
| **Part I** | Preliminary (Interpretation and Definitions) |
| **Part II** | The Pharmacy Council |
| **Part III** | Registration of Pharmacists, Pharmaceutical Students, etc. |
| **Part IV** | Compounding, Dispensing, Storing for Sale and Retailing |
| **First Schedule** | Constitution of the Pharmacy Council |
| **Second Schedule** | Provisions relating to the Registration Appeal Tribunal |

**Key Provisions:**

**Registration Requirements (Part III):**
- All pharmacists must be registered with the Pharmacy Council of Jamaica
- Foreign-trained pharmacists may apply if they can demonstrate their qualification is equivalent to or higher than that required in Jamaica and they have undergone equivalent practical training
- Registration requires: 3 testimonials (2 from registered pharmacists, 1 other), registration fee of $50 USD (or JMD equivalent), 2 certified passport-size photographs
- Vocational training = 12 months in an approved hospital or pharmacy

**Pharmacy Operational Requirements (Part IV):**
- All pharmacies must be registered with the Pharmacy Council
- A registered pharmacy must be under the **immediate control, management and supervision of a registered pharmacist**
- Pharmacy cannot be open unless a registered pharmacist is in charge AND in actual attendance, OR all drugs requiring pharmacist supervision are put away and properly secured
- Both the pharmacy's Certificate of Registration and the pharmacist's certificate must be conspicuously displayed
- It is an **offence** to display on unregistered premises any sign or emblem saying "drugs," "drugstore," "pharmacy," etc.

**Poisons Restrictions:**
- It is an offence to sell any kind of poison unless the business is registered as a pharmacy OR the person is an authorized seller of poisons
- All poisons must be labeled showing: the word "poison," the name of the poison, the seller's name, and business address

**Penalties:**
- Violations result in fines or imprisonment (specific amounts set by the Act and amendments)

- https://www.cac.gov.jm/portal/index.php/consumer-laws/79-the-laws/113-pharmacy-act

### 1.2 The Food and Drugs Act, 1964 & Regulations, 1975

**Purpose:** Authorizes the regulation of foods, drugs, cosmetics, and medical devices for both domestically manufactured and imported products.

**Drug Classification System:**

Jamaica classifies drugs using a **List system** under the Food and Drugs Regulations:

| Classification | Description | Dispensing Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| **List 1 Drugs** | Drugs classified by the Pharmacy Council's List Committee; available for sale at registered pharmacies | Pharmacy sale only; does not require prescription |
| **List 2 Drugs** | Drugs classified by the Pharmacy Council's List Committee; more restricted than List 1 | Pharmacy sale only with enhanced oversight |
| **List 4 Drugs (Fourth Schedule)** | Prescription-only medications | **Must not be retailed except on or in accordance with a prescription from a practitioner** |

**Note:** The Pharmacy Council of Jamaica's **List Committee** reviews pharmaceutical products submitted from the Ministry of Health and Wellness for classification into List 1 or List 2 drugs. List 4 drugs are established under the Food and Drugs Regulations Fourth Schedule.

**List 4 (Prescription-Only) Drug Rules:**
- Cannot be advertised to the general public
- Cannot be sold without a prescription (written or verbal)
- Written prescriptions must be retained for **at least 2 years** from date of filling
- Verbal prescriptions must be reduced to writing **immediately** and validated by the prescriber within **36 hours**; then retained for 2 years
- Repeat dispensing: each repeat must be recorded on the original prescription with date and quantity

**Generic Substitution Requirement:**
- Where a prescription names a specific List 4 Drug and a bioequivalent generic exists that is less costly, the registered pharmacist **must inform the patient** that:
  - A bioequivalent generic is available
  - It is less costly
  - The generic will be dispensed unless the patient objects or the prescriber forbids substitution

**New Drug Registration:**
- All new drugs require ministerial licensing before importation or sale
- Must demonstrate safety and efficacy
- Manufacturer certification required

**Product Registration with Ministry of Health:**
- Required for: pharmaceuticals (Rx and OTC), herbal preparations, natural health products, vitamins and minerals, cosmetics/foods/devices making therapeutic claims
- Products deemed safe enough may receive written approval without formal registration

**Labeling Requirements for All Drugs:**
- Proper or common name
- Manufacturer/distributor details
- Quantitative ingredient list (except prescriptions)
- Adequate use directions
- Net contents
- Lot numbers (for parenteral use)
- Preservative information (for parenteral use)
- Specific warnings required for salicylic acid (children) and corticosteroid ophthalmic products

**Prescription Definition (from PSJ):**
- "A lawful written instruction from a licensed physician or other medical practitioner to a licensed pharmacist regarding the compounding or dispensing and administration of drug/s or other medical services to the patient"

## 2. PHARMACY COUNCIL OF JAMAICA (PCOJ)

### 2.1 Establishment and Authority
- Established in **August 1975** by the Ministry of Health under the Pharmacy Act, 1966
- Statutory body created under Part II of the Pharmacy Act
- Website: https://pcoj.gov.jm/

### 2.2 Composition
- **10 members total:**
  - **6 members** elected by the Pharmaceutical Society of Jamaica (PSJ)
  - **4 members** (including the Chairman) appointed by the **Minister of Health and Wellness**
- Minister's appointees often come from: consumer groups, pharmaceutical distribution sector, other healthcare disciplines, legal profession, or regulatory framework

### 2.3 Functions (as defined by the Pharmacy Act)
1. **Register** pharmacists, pharmaceutical students, pharmacies, and owners of pharmacies
2. **Regulate** the training of pharmaceutical students
3. **Register** persons as authorized sellers of poisons
4. **Ensure** maintenance of proper standards of conduct by persons registered under the Act
5. **Ensure** compliance with the requirements of the Act
6. **Maintain registers** for all registered individuals and entities
7. **Set standards** for pharmacists' education, scopes of practice, and professional conduct
8. **Set and maintain standards** for pharmacy establishment and operations

### 2.4 Committees
- **List Committee** -- Reviews pharmaceutical products submitted from the Ministry of Health and Wellness for classification into List 1 or List 2 drugs
- Other committees as needed for governance

### 2.5 Meeting Schedule
- Meets **monthly**, except August and December
- Frequent schedule designed to address the needs of the rapidly expanding pharmaceutical industry

### 2.6 Disciplinary Authority
- The Pharmacy Council is the **arbiter of what constitutes misconduct**
- A breach of the Code of Ethics can form the basis of a complaint of misconduct
- The Council considers circumstances of individual cases and is not limited to matters mentioned in the Code
- Appeals from Council decisions go to the **Registration Appeal Tribunal** (established under the Second Schedule of the Pharmacy Act)

### 2.7 Registration Process for Pharmacists
**Requirements:**
- Recognized pharmacy qualification (Pharm.D. from UWI Mona or equivalent)
- Foreign-trained applicants must demonstrate equivalent qualification and practical training
- **12 months vocational training** (internship) in an approved hospital or pharmacy
- 3 testimonials (2 from registered pharmacists, 1 other)
- Registration fee: $50 USD or JMD equivalent
- 2 passport-size photographs certified by a Justice of the Peace

**Internship Requirements (per PCOJ Internship Manual 2023):**
- 100 hours of rotation
- Must observe all provisions of the Pharmacy Act and Regulations
- Must comply with the Code of Ethics
- Experience must include: dispensing, compounding, medication inventory management, insurance processing and auditing, patient monitoring, and exposure to common clinical disciplines

## 3. SCOPE OF PRACTICE

### 3.1 Pharmacists -- Exclusive Activities (Restricted to Registered Pharmacists)
Under the Pharmacy Act:
- **Compounding** of drugs and poisons
- **Dispensing** of prescription medications
- **Supervision** of all pharmacy operations (must be in actual attendance when pharmacy is open)
- **Management and control** of registered pharmacies
- **Sale of poisons** (only at registered pharmacies or by authorized sellers)
- **Counseling** patients on List 4 drugs and generic substitution
- Professional judgment on generic bioequivalent substitution

### 3.2 Pharmacy Technicians -- Scope of Practice

**IMPORTANT REGULATORY NOTE:** Jamaica's Pharmacy Act (1966) does **not specifically define or regulate "pharmacy technicians"** as a separate professional category. The Act regulates pharmacists, pharmaceutical students, pharmacy owners, and authorized sellers of poisons. Pharmacy technicians operate under the **direct supervision of registered pharmacists** in a delegated capacity.

**What Pharmacy Technicians Typically Do in Jamaica (Under Pharmacist Supervision):**
- Assist in dispensing prescription medications under pharmacist supervision
- Measure and count medication quantities
- Mix or compound some medicines under supervision
- Package and label prescriptions
- Collect patient information and process payments
- Process insurance claims (including NHF)
- Take inventory and note shortages
- Operate registers and provide customer service
- Locate and retrieve over-the-counter medications
- Call doctors for prescription refill authorization
- Prepare certain preparations (e.g., IV medications in hospital settings, under supervision)

**What Pharmacy Technicians CANNOT Do (Reserved for Pharmacists):**
- Independently compound medications
- Independently dispense prescription drugs without pharmacist verification
- Provide clinical drug counseling to patients
- Make decisions on generic substitution
- Operate a pharmacy without a pharmacist present
- Sell poisons without proper authorization
- Sign off on controlled substance records independently

## 4. CONTROLLED SUBSTANCES: THE DANGEROUS DRUGS ACT

### 4.1 Structure of the Dangerous Drugs Act (1948)

| Part | Subject | Key Sections |
|---|---|---|
| **Part I** | Interpretation | Definitions of cocaine leaves, medicinal opium, prepared opium, ganja; references to Hague Convention and Geneva Conventions |
| **Part II** | Raw Opium and Coca Leaves | Sections 3-5: importation, exportation, cultivation |
| **Part III** | Prepared Opium | Sections 6-7: manufacture, sale, possession, smoking |
| **Part IIIA** | Ganja (Cannabis) | Sections 7A-7D: specific cannabis regulations (added by amendments) |
| **Part IV** | Cocaine, Morphine, etc. | Sections 8-11: manufacture, sale, possession |
| **Part V** | Control of External Trade | Sections 12-18: import/export licensing, authorizations, certificates, transit controls |
| **Part VI** | General | Sections 19-28: licensing terms, inspection powers, seizure authority, postal offences, evidence |

### 4.2 Drug Classifications Under the Dangerous Drugs Act

| Category | Drugs Covered |
|---|---|
| **Raw Opium & Coca Leaves** | Opium poppy, coca plant and leaves |
| **Prepared Opium** | Processed opium for smoking/consumption |
| **Ganja (Cannabis)** | Cannabis sativa plant with resin intact, cannabis extracts |
| **Cocaine, Morphine, etc.** | Cocaine, morphine, heroin (diacetylmorphine), ecgonine, related compounds |

### 4.3 Penalties

| Offence | Penalty (Circuit Court) | Penalty (Magistrate) |
|---|---|---|
| Cultivation of opium poppy or coca plant | Up to 35 years imprisonment or $500,000 fine | Up to 5 years or fine |
| Ganja export/import (s.7A) | Minimum $500/ounce or 35 years | As prescribed |
| Ganja cultivation/dealing (s.7B) | Minimum $200/ounce or 35 years | As prescribed |
| Ganja possession (s.7C) | Up to $100/ounce or 3 years | As prescribed |
| Ganja smoking/use (s.7D) | First offence: up to $5,000 or 12 months | As prescribed |
| Cocaine/morphine offences (Part IV) | Up to 35 years imprisonment | Up to 5 years or $500,000 fine |
| Postal drug violations | 15 years imprisonment | Up to 5 years |

**Trafficking Presumption Thresholds:**
- Heroin/cocaine/morphine: 0.1 ounce
- Opium: 1 ounce
- Ganja: 8 ounces
- Possession on school premises: automatically presumed for sale

### 4.4 Authorized Persons (Who Can Handle Controlled Substances)

Under the Dangerous Drugs Regulations, the following are authorized to possess drugs within their professional scope:
- Registered medical practitioners
- Registered dentists
- Laboratory personnel at approved institutions
- Government dispensers and chemists
- Authorized veterinary surgeons
- Analysts designated under the Food and Drugs Act
- **Persons lawfully keeping open shop for the retailing of poisons under the Pharmacy Act** (i.e., registered pharmacies)

Medical practitioners, government dispensers, and veterinary surgeons may also **supply** drugs as part of their professional practice.

### 4.5 Prescription Requirements for Dangerous Drugs

Prescriptions for dangerous drugs must be:
- **Written**, signed with the prescriber's usual signature, and dated
- Limited to **14 days' supply**
- Marked "**For local dental treatment only**" (dentists) or "**For animal treatment only**" (veterinarians)
- Specify the recipient's **name and address**
- Include **quantity specifications** appropriate to the preparation type
- Suppliers must **verify prescriptions are genuine** before dispensing

### 4.6 Record-Keeping Requirements

- Records must be kept in **bound books** (not loose-leaf registers or card indexes)
- Entries must be made **in ink**
- Separate sections for each class of drugs and preparations
- Must record both **obtaining and supply** details
- Records must be retained for **at least 2 years**
- Records must be available for **inspection upon demand** by authorized officers

### 4.7 Storage Requirements

- All dangerous drugs in the custody of an authorized person must be kept in a **locked receptacle**
- The locked receptacle can only be opened by the authorized person or by an assistant who is a **chemist and druggist** (i.e., registered pharmacist)

### 4.8 Enforcement Powers

Police and authorized medical officials may:
- Enter business premises and inspect records/stocks
- Search premises with written directions from Sergeant+ rank
- Obtain judicial search warrants
- Seize drugs, equipment, and conveyances
- Arrest without warrant for suspected offences
- The Minister may withdraw authorization from convicted persons by Gazette notice

### 4.9 The Dangerous Drugs (Amendment) Act, 2015 -- "The Ganja Law"

**Effective Date:** April 15, 2015

**Key Provisions:**

**Decriminalization of Personal Possession:**
- Possession of **2 ounces (57g) or less** of cannabis is now a **non-arrestable, ticketable offence** that attracts **no criminal record**
- Police may issue a ticket; person has **30 days to pay J$500** at any Tax Office
- Possession of **more than 2 ounces** remains a criminal offence with arrest, trial, and criminal record

**Rastafarian Religious Use:**
- Adherents to the Rastafarian faith permitted to smoke ganja for sacramental purposes in **registered places of Rastafarian worship**
- Persons 18+ who are Rastafarian adherents may apply for authorization to cultivate ganja for religious purposes

**Home Cultivation:**
- Each household may legally grow **no more than 5 ganja plants** on its premises

**Public Smoking:**
- Smoking ganja in a public place or within **5 metres of a public place** is prohibited (similar to cigarette restrictions)

**Medical/Therapeutic/Scientific Use:**
- Establishes framework for lawful medical, therapeutic, and scientific use
- Creates a regulated industry for ganja and hemp

**Hemp Definition:**
- Hemp = ganja plant having a **THC content of less than 1%**

**Cannabis Licensing Authority (CLA):**
- Established in 2015 under the DDA as an agency of the Ministry of Industry, Commerce, Agriculture and Fisheries
- Role: establish and regulate Jamaica's legal ganja and hemp industry
- Issues licences, permits, and authorizations for handling of hemp and marijuana for medical, therapeutic, or scientific purposes
- Website: https://www.cla.org.jm/

**CLA Licence Types include:**
- Cultivator licences
- Processor/manufacturer licences
- Retail (dispensary) licences -- authorized to sell ganja products in specified quantities for medical, scientific, and therapeutic purposes
- Transport licences
- Research/analytical licences

**Pharmacy Connection:** In accordance with the Pharmacy Act, the compounding, dispensing, storing for sale or retailing of drugs (including cannabis-based products) must be done at a registered pharmacy and by a registered pharmacist.

## 5. PATIENT CONFIDENTIALITY & DATA PROTECTION

### 5.1 The Data Protection Act, 2020

**Effective Date:** December 1, 2020

**Purpose:** Provides greater safeguards for the handling of personal information of Jamaicans held in physical or electronic form, transforming the way organizations manage personal data including collection, storage, utilisation, disclosure, and disposal.

### 5.2 The Eight Data Protection Standards (Principles)

All data controllers (including pharmacies and healthcare providers) must comply:

| # | Principle | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Fair and Lawful Processing** | Personal data must be processed fairly and lawfully; must not be obtained by deception or misleading information; must have a legitimate reason for processing |
| 2 | **Informed Consent** | Data subject must expressly consent; consent must be informed, freely given, specific, and unequivocal |
| 3 | **Information to Data Subjects** | Data subject must receive all relevant information about processing to enable informed decision-making |
| 4 | **Purpose Limitation** | Purpose must be specified before collection; data cannot be used for other purposes without informing and (where necessary) receiving consent |
| 5 | **Data Quality** | Data must be adequate, relevant, limited to what is necessary, accurate, and kept up to date |
| 6 | **Retention Limitation** | Data must not be kept longer than necessary; must be disposed of in accordance with regulations |
| 7 | **Rights of Data Subjects** | Data must be processed in accordance with data subject's rights, including right to access and right to prevent processing in specified circumstances |
| 8 | **Security** | Data must be protected using appropriate technical and organizational measures to prevent unauthorized/unlawful processing and accidental loss/destruction/damage |

### 5.3 Application to Pharmacy/Healthcare

- **Health information is classified as "sensitive personal data"** under the Act (alongside biometric data, political/religious ideas, and sexual orientation)
- Processing of sensitive data requires **written consent** from the data subject
- Entities processing sensitive personal data (including pharmacies handling patient health records) must appoint a **Data Protection Officer (DPO)**
- Electronic transmission of prescriptions must meet the **legal requirements of the Data Protection Act 2020** concerning end-to-end operations to safeguard patient data privacy and security (per Ministry of Health guidance)
- All data controllers must be **registered with the Office of the Information Commissioner (OIC)**

### 5.4 Enforcement

- **Oversight body:** Office of the Information Commissioner (OIC)
- OIC monitors compliance and advises Government on data protection matters

**Penalties for Non-Compliance:**
- Up to **4% of annual gross revenue** for the preceding year
- Personal liability
- Monetary fine of up to **J$5 million**
- Term of imprisonment of up to **10 years**

### 5.5 Pharmacy-Specific Confidentiality (from Code of Ethics)

The PSJ Code of Ethics provides an additional layer:
- "A pharmacist must **respect the confidentiality** of information acquired in the course of professional practice relating to patients and their families"
- "Such information must **not be disclosed to anyone** without the consent of the patient or appropriate guardian unless the **interest of the public or the patient** requires such disclosure"

## 6. ETHICS IN PHARMACY PRACTICE

### 6.1 PSJ Code of Ethics

**Issuing Body:** Pharmaceutical Society of Jamaica (PSJ)
**Structure:** Two parts with **9 Principles** supplemented by detailed **Obligations** and supported by **Guidance** for interpretation.

**Scope:** Sets the standard of professional conduct for all pharmacists and "persons lawfully conducting a retail pharmacy business" within the meaning of the Pharmacy Act.

### 6.2 Key Principles and Obligations

Based on available sources, the following principles and obligations are documented:

**Principle: Professional Competence**
- A pharmacist must **keep abreast of the progress of pharmaceutical knowledge** in order to maintain a high standard of professional competence relative to his sphere of activity

**Principle: Legal Compliance and Professional Standards**
- A pharmacist must at all times have regard to the **laws and regulations applicable to pharmaceutical practice** and maintain a high standard of professional conduct

**Principle: Professional Independence**
- A pharmacist must **neither agree to practice under any conditions of service which compromise his professional independence** nor impose such conditions on other pharmacists

**Principle: Avoiding Discredit to the Profession**
- A pharmacist must avoid any act or omission which would **impair confidence in the pharmaceutical profession**

**Principle: Service Efficiency**
- When a pharmaceutical service is provided, a pharmacist must ensure that it is **efficient**

**Principle: Confidentiality**
- A pharmacist must respect the **confidentiality of information** acquired in the course of professional practice relating to patients and their families
- Information must not be disclosed without consent unless the interest of the public or patient requires it

**Principle: Cooperation with Colleagues**
- A pharmacist must at all times endeavor to **cooperate with professional colleagues and members of other health professions** so that patients and the public may benefit

**Principle: Professional Premises Standards**
- A pharmacist offering services directly to the public must do so in premises which **reflect the professional character of pharmacy** in keeping with the regulations set by the Pharmacy Council of Jamaica

### 6.3 Enforcement of Ethics

- A **breach of the Code of Ethics** could form the basis of a complaint of misconduct
- The Council considers the **circumstances of each individual case** and does not limit itself to matters mentioned in the Code
- The **arbiter of what constitutes misconduct** is the Pharmacy Council
- Appeals from Council decisions lie to the **Registration Appeal Tribunal**

### 6.4 The Pharmaceutical Society of Jamaica (PSJ)

- Originally established as the **Jamaica Pharmacists' Association**; renamed to PSJ in 1944
- Articles of Association entered in July 1948
- Mission: maintain the dignity of the profession by promoting high standards of conduct in all aspects of professional practice
- **6 of 10** Pharmacy Council members are elected by the PSJ, giving it significant influence in regulation
- Website: https://psjjamaica.org/

**Sources:**
- https://psjjamaica.org/psj-code-of-ethics/
- https://psjjamaica.org/about-us/

## 7. DRUG CLASSIFICATION: OTC VS PRESCRIPTION

### 7.1 Jamaica's Drug Classification Framework

Jamaica uses a **multi-layered classification system** governed by different legislation for different drug categories:

**Layer 1: Food and Drugs Act/Regulations -- Drug Lists**

| Classification | Legal Basis | Who Can Sell | Prescription Required? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **General Sale / Household Remedies** | Food and Drugs Act | Available at retail outlets (not just pharmacies) | No |
| **List 1 Drugs** | Classified by PCOJ List Committee | Registered pharmacies only | No (pharmacy sale) |
| **List 2 Drugs** | Classified by PCOJ List Committee | Registered pharmacies with enhanced oversight | No (pharmacy sale, restricted) |
| **List 4 Drugs (Fourth Schedule)** | Food and Drugs Regulations | Registered pharmacies only | **Yes -- prescription only** |

**Layer 2: Pharmacy Act -- Poisons**

| Category | Who Can Sell |
|---|---|
| **Part I Poisons** | Only at registered pharmacies under pharmacist supervision |
| **Part II Poisons** | Registered pharmacies OR authorized sellers of poisons |

**Layer 3: Dangerous Drugs Act -- Controlled Substances**

Covered substances (opium, cocaine, morphine, heroin, ganja) have the most restrictive controls -- see Section 4 above.

### 7.2 Product Registration

All pharmaceutical products require registration with the **Ministry of Health** before importation, distribution, and sale in Jamaica:
- Pharmaceuticals (prescription and OTC)
- Herbal preparations
- Natural health products
- Products containing vitamins and minerals
- Cosmetics, foods, or devices making therapeutic claims
- Some products deemed safe may receive written approval without formal registration

### 7.3 The VEN List (Vital, Essential, and Necessary)

Used for the **public sector** (not a scheduling system per se, but fundamental to practice):
- Maintained by the Ministry of Health
- Basis for **public sector procurement** and **public insurance reimbursement**
- Over **1,000 drug categories** (~60 treating each covered ailment)
- Available at Drug Serv pharmacies and Public Sector Partner Pharmacies via the **GOJ Health Card**
- VEN classification is key to the rational drug use framework

## 8. CARICOM CONTEXT: JAMAICA'S ROLE IN PHARMACEUTICAL HARMONIZATION

### 8.1 Caribbean Pharmaceutical Policy (2013)
- Adopted by CARICOM Ministers of Health in 2011 (finalized 2013)
- Overarching policy framework calling for regionwide regulatory cooperation
- Underpins the establishment of the Caribbean Regulatory System

### 8.2 Caribbean Regulatory System (CRS)
- Managed as a regulatory unit within **CARPHA** (Caribbean Public Health Agency)
- Located in **Trinidad and Tobago**
- Serves all **15 CARICOM Member States** (including Jamaica)
- Operationalized in **2017**

**Core Functions:**
1. **Product Reviews** -- Provides recommendations for market authorization or import of medical products
2. **Vigilance** -- Monitoring quality and safety of medical products (through VigiCarib)

**How It Works:**
- Uses a **reliance approach** leveraging decisions by reference authorities (US FDA, Health Canada, EMA)
- Focus on **essential medicines**
- Target time for scientific assessment: **90 days**
- Questions to applicants trigger clock stops; applicants have **14 days** to respond
- CRS publishes a list of recommended products; each country then decides within **2 months** whether to grant registration
- **CRS Recommended Medicines Registry**: https://crsregistry.carpha.org

**Jamaica's Specific Approach:**
- Jamaica **may build on CRS regulatory work** with additional local requirements
- Unlike some countries (e.g., Guyana, which grants immediate recognition), Jamaica maintains its own review process supplemented by CRS
- The **Kingston CARPHA lab** (operational since 1980) serves CARPHA members by risk-based selection of medicines for market monitoring

### 8.3 Regional Procurement
- **OECS Pharmaceutical Procurement Service** -- Partnerships with CRS for Eastern Caribbean procurement
- Jamaica uses **NHF** for its own pharmaceutical procurement (see Section 9)
- CARICOM member states benefit from pooled procurement negotiations

### 8.4 Pan American Network for Drug Regulatory Harmonization (PANDRH)
- Jamaica participates through PAHO/WHO coordination
- Promotes convergence of pharmaceutical regulatory standards across the Americas

### 8.5 VigiCarib (Regional Pharmacovigilance)
- Regional online reporting system for adverse drug reactions (ADRs) and substandard/falsified products
- Launched 2017 by CARPHA
- Connected to WHO Uppsala Monitoring Centre
- Healthcare professionals, consumers, and patients can report ADR events

**Guiding Principles of CRS:**
- Efficiency, transparency, resource-sharing, and capacity-building
- Contact: CRSRegistration@carpha.org

## 9. HEALTH SYSTEM STRUCTURE: PUBLIC VS PRIVATE PHARMACY

### 9.1 Overview of Jamaica's Health System

Jamaica operates a **mixed public-private healthcare system** with significant pharmaceutical sector involvement from both sectors.

**Key Statistic:** Private funds currently finance approximately **82% of pharmaceutical costs** in Jamaica.

### 9.2 Public Sector Pharmacy

**Drug Serv Pharmacies:**
- Public sector pharmacies operated by the **National Health Fund (NHF)**
- Located at **government-run hospitals and selected health clinics**
- Currently **106 Drug Serv Pharmacy locations** across the island (full-time and scheduled service)
- Dispense medications from the **VEN List** (Vital, Essential, and Necessary drugs)

**The GOJ Health Card:**
- When presented with a **public sector prescription** at Drug Serv pharmacies or Public Sector Partner Pharmacies, entitles users to VEN list drugs
- Provides access to **all health services and medication** on the VEN list at **no out-of-pocket cost**

### 9.3 The National Health Fund (NHF)

**Establishment:** 2003, under the National Health Fund Act

**Purpose:** Contributory health insurance system to benefit all residents of Jamaica; provides direct assistance to patients for drug purchases and funding support to organizations for approved projects.

**Key Programs:**

**NHF Individual Benefits (NHF Card):**
- Persons must be certified by a registered doctor with one or more specified medical conditions
- Once registered, issued an **NHF Card**
- NHF makes a **fixed payment** toward the price set by the pharmacy for approved drugs
- Beneficiary pays the **co-payment** (difference)
- Can be used at **approved participating pharmacies** (public and private)
- Over **1,000 drug categories** covered

**NHF Public-Private Sector Pharmacy Partner Programme:**
- NHF partners with **select private pharmacies** to dispense NHF-owned drugs to public patients
- Increases access options beyond public sector Drug Serv locations
- Currently over **100 private pharmacies** participate in public programs

**JADEP (Jamaica Drugs for the Elderly Programme):**
- Launched in **1996**
- Specifically designed to alleviate hardships experienced by elderly clients in obtaining medicines

**NHF Supply Chain Role (since 2011):**
- NHF assumed responsibilities for **procurement, warehousing, and distribution** of pharmaceuticals and medical sundries for government health facilities throughout the island
- Technical support from PAHO to strengthen supply chain systems

### 9.4 Private Sector Pharmacy

- Private pharmacies form the majority of pharmacy outlets
- Regulated under the Pharmacy Act (must be registered, pharmacist must be present)
- Many participate in NHF programs as partner pharmacies
- Pharmacy trade depends heavily on **imported drugs**
- Operators rely on third-party distributors for OTC medicines and prescription drugs

### 9.5 Pharmaceutical Workforce Challenges

- **Brain drain**: Qualified pharmacists constantly migrating to Canada, USA, Bermuda, Cayman Islands
- **Job vacancies**: Positions going unfilled; small operators cannot compete on salary
- **Drug pricing**: High drug prices due to import dependency; operators have little control over product prices
- **No adopted National Pharmaceutical Policy**: Jamaica does not have an officially adopted National Pharmaceutical Policy, though efforts have been made to develop one

### 9.6 Key Health System Statistics

- Jamaica has a population of approximately 2.8 million
- Public health sector includes regional hospitals, parish hospitals, health centres
- Ministry of Health and Wellness oversees public health regulation through the **Standards and Regulation Division** and **Pharmaceutical and Regulatory Affairs Department**

## 10. THE PRECURSOR CHEMICALS ACT, 1999

- Operational since **December 3, 2013**
- Provides for monitoring and control of **precursor chemicals** and other chemical substances used or capable of being used in illicit operations involving narcotic drugs, psychotropic substances, or similar substances
- The pharmaceutical division is responsible for:
  - Monitoring, controlling, and investigating matters relating to precursor chemicals
  - Ensuring surveillance systems on movement of specified chemical substances into/out of Jamaica
  - Granting licenses
  - Promoting public awareness of potential illicit use
- The **Pharmaceutical Society of Jamaica** has advocated for updates to this Act

**Source:** https://laws.moj.gov.jm/library/subsidiary-legislation/the-precursor-chemicals-act

## 12. IMPORTANT CAVEATS FOR LMS COURSE DEVELOPMENT

1. **Pharmacy Technician Regulation Gap**: The Pharmacy Act (1966) does not specifically define or regulate pharmacy technicians. This is a recognized gap. Course content should emphasize that technicians work under pharmacist supervision and that their role is defined by delegation rather than specific legislation.

2. **Outdated Legislation**: The Pharmacy Act dates to 1966 and may not reflect modern pharmacy practice realities. The Pharmaceutical Society of Jamaica has advocated for legislative updates.

3. **Drug Classification Details**: The full details of Lists 1 and 2 drug classification criteria are maintained by the Pharmacy Council's List Committee and may not be publicly available in full online. Course developers should consider requesting official documentation from PCOJ.

4. **CPD Requirements**: Specific continuing professional development (CPD) hour requirements for pharmacist registration renewal in Jamaica are not well-documented in publicly available sources. This should be verified with PCOJ directly.

5. **Cannabis Law Complexity**: The cannabis regulatory landscape in Jamaica is evolving. The intersection between the Dangerous Drugs Act, the CLA licensing framework, and the Pharmacy Act creates unique considerations for pharmacy practice.

6. **No National Pharmaceutical Policy**: Jamaica has not officially adopted a National Pharmaceutical Policy, unlike some other CARICOM nations.
