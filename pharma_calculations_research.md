### Key Concepts to Teach

**1. Fractions**
- Proper fractions, improper fractions, mixed numbers
- Reducing fractions to lowest terms
- Adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing fractions
- Converting between fractions and decimals

**2. Decimals**
- Place values (tenths, hundredths, thousandths)
- Adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing decimals
- Rounding rules for pharmacy:
  - Tablets/capsules: round to nearest whole number (capsules) or half-tablet (scored tablets)
  - Liquids: round to nearest tenth

- Percent strength in pharmacy solutions (e.g., 0.9% NS = 0.9 g NaCl per 100 mL)

- Reading and writing Roman numerals (commonly used on prescriptions)

## MODULE 2: SYSTEMS OF MEASUREMENT & UNIT CONVERSIONS
### Metric, Apothecary, Household

### Key Concepts to Teach

**1. The Metric System (International System of Units)**
- Base units: gram (g) for weight, liter (L) for volume, meter (m) for length
- Prefixes and their values:
  - kilo- (k) = 1000 (1 kg = 1000 g)
  - deci- (d) = 0.1
  - centi- (c) = 0.01
  - milli- (m) = 0.001 (1 g = 1000 mg)
  - micro- (mc or mcg) = 0.000001 (1 mg = 1000 mcg)
- Moving the decimal point for conversions within the metric system
- The metric system is the PRIMARY system used in Caribbean pharmacies

**2. The Apothecary System (Historical)**
- Largely phased out but still encountered on some prescriptions
- Key units: grain (gr), dram, ounce, minim
- Key conversions:
  - 1 grain (gr) = 64.8 mg (approximately 65 mg)
  - Aspirin gr v = 325 mg
  - Paracetamol gr viiss = 500 mg (approximately)
- Roman numerals used with apothecary measures

**3. The Household System**
- Used for patient counseling and home medication administration
- Key units: teaspoon (tsp), tablespoon (tbsp), cup, ounce, pound
- Commonly used in Caribbean homes for measuring liquid medications

**4. Temperature Conversions**
- Celsius to Fahrenheit: F = (C x 9/5) + 32
- Fahrenheit to Celsius: C = (F - 32) x 5/9
- Normal body temperature: 37 degrees C = 98.6 degrees F
- Important for medication storage requirements

**5. Key Inter-System Conversions**

## MODULE 3: DOSAGE CALCULATIONS
### Oral Solids, Liquids & Injectables

### Key Concepts to Teach

**1. Three Methods of Dosage Calculation**

*Method A: Desired Over Have (Formula Method)*
- Formula: D/H x Q = X
  - D = Desired dose (what is ordered)
  - H = Have (what is on hand/available)
  - Q = Quantity (dosage form -- tablet, mL, etc.)
  - X = Amount to administer

*Method B: Ratio and Proportion*
- Setup: H/Q = D/X
- Cross multiply and solve for X
- Ensure units match on both sides before cross-multiplying

*Method C: Dimensional Analysis (Factor-Label Method)*
- Chain of conversion factors that cancel units until desired unit remains
- Most versatile method, can handle multi-step conversions in a single equation

**2. Oral Solid Dosage Calculations**
- Tablets: scored vs. unscored
- Capsules: cannot be divided
- Calculating number of tablets or capsules per dose
- Calculating total quantity for a prescription

**3. Oral Liquid Dosage Calculations**
- Solutions, suspensions, syrups, elixirs
- Reading drug concentration labels (e.g., 250 mg/5 mL)
- Calculating volume per dose
- Measuring devices: oral syringes, medicine cups, droppers

**4. Injectable Dosage Calculations**
- Intramuscular (IM) and subcutaneous (SC) injections
- Reading vial labels and concentrations
- Calculating volume to draw up in syringe
- Reconstitution of powdered medications
  - Reading reconstitution instructions on labels
  - Calculating resulting concentration after reconstitution
  - Common diluents: sterile water for injection, bacteriostatic water, 0.9% NS

**5. Days' Supply Calculations**
- Formula: Days' Supply = Total Quantity Dispensed / (Dose x Frequency per day)
- Used for insurance billing and refill scheduling

## MODULE 4: PEDIATRIC & WEIGHT-BASED DOSING
### Calculations for Children and Weight-Dependent Doses

### Key Concepts and Formulas

**1. Clark's Rule (Weight-Based, Ages 2-17)**
```
Child's Dose = Adult Dose x (Weight of child in lbs / 150)
```
- Uses the child's weight in pounds
- If weight in kg, convert first: kg x 2.2 = lbs
- More accurate than age-based methods
- 150 lbs represents the assumed average adult weight

**2. Young's Rule (Age-Based, Ages 2-12)**
```
Child's Dose = Adult Dose x [Age in years / (Age + 12)]
```
- Uses the child's age in years only
- Quick method when weight is unknown
- Less accurate than weight-based methods

**3. Fried's Rule (Infants < 1 year)**
```
Infant's Dose = Adult Dose x (Age in months / 150)
```
- For infants under 12 months of age
- Uses age in months
- Least precise method; used when no other data available

**4. Body Surface Area (BSA) Method**

*Mosteller Formula for BSA:*
```
BSA (m2) = Square root of [(Height in cm x Weight in kg) / 3600]
```

*BSA-Based Dose:*
```
Child's Dose = (BSA of child in m2 / 1.73 m2) x Adult Dose
```
- 1.73 m2 = average adult BSA
- Most accurate method
- Required for chemotherapy and other critical medications
- Haycock formula preferred for neonates/infants

**5. mg/kg Dosing (Modern Standard)**
```
Dose = Patient weight (kg) x Dose per kg (mg/kg)
```
- Most commonly used method in modern practice
- Used for antibiotics, analgesics, anti-epileptics
- Many drug references provide mg/kg dosing ranges
- Example: Amoxicillin 25-50 mg/kg/day divided into 3 doses

**6. Weight Considerations in Dosing**
- Total Body Weight (TBW): actual measured weight
- Ideal Body Weight (IBW):
  - Males: 50 kg + 2.3 kg for each inch over 5 feet
  - Females: 45.5 kg + 2.3 kg for each inch over 5 feet
- Adjusted Body Weight: IBW + 0.4(TBW - IBW)
  - Used for obese patients, especially for aminoglycosides

## MODULE 5: IV FLOW RATES, DRIP RATES & INFUSION TIME
### Calculations for Intravenous Therapy

### Key Concepts and Formulas

**1. IV Flow Rate (mL/hr)**
```
Flow Rate (mL/hr) = Total Volume (mL) / Infusion Time (hr)
```

**2. Infusion Time**
```
Infusion Time (hr) = Total Volume (mL) / Flow Rate (mL/hr)
```

**3. Total Volume**
```
Total Volume (mL) = Flow Rate (mL/hr) x Infusion Time (hr)
```

**4. IV Drip Rate (drops per minute)**
```
Drip Rate (gtt/min) = [Total Volume (mL) / Time (min)] x Drop Factor (gtt/mL)
```
Or equivalently:
```
Drip Rate (gtt/min) = [Total Volume (mL) x Drop Factor (gtt/mL)] / Time (min)
```

**5. Drop Factors by Tubing Type**

| Tubing Type | Drop Factor | Use |
|------------|-------------|-----|
| Macrodrip (standard) | 10 gtt/mL | Large volume infusions |
| Macrodrip | 15 gtt/mL | General use, blood products |
| Macrodrip | 20 gtt/mL | General use, clear fluids |
| Microdrip | 60 gtt/mL | Pediatric, critical medications |

**6. Shortcut Formulas for Macrodrip**
- For 10 gtt/mL sets: gtt/min = mL/hr / 6
- For 15 gtt/mL sets: gtt/min = mL/hr / 4
- For 20 gtt/mL sets: gtt/min = mL/hr / 3

**7. Microdrip Shortcut**
- For 60 gtt/mL sets: gtt/min = mL/hr (they are equal!)

**8. IV Medication Dose Rate**
```
Dose Rate (mg/hr) = Concentration (mg/mL) x Flow Rate (mL/hr)
```

**9. Common IV Solutions**
- Normal Saline (NS or 0.9% NaCl): 0.9 g NaCl per 100 mL
- Half Normal Saline (1/2 NS or 0.45% NaCl)
- D5W (5% Dextrose in Water): 5 g dextrose per 100 mL
- D5NS (5% Dextrose in Normal Saline)
- Lactated Ringer's (LR or RL)

## MODULE 6: COMPOUNDING CALCULATIONS
### Dilutions, Concentrations & Alligation

### Key Concepts and Formulas

**1. Concentration Expressions**

| Expression | Formula | Use Case |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| % w/v | grams of solute per 100 mL solution | Solids dissolved in liquids |
| % v/v | mL of solute per 100 mL solution | Liquid in liquid |
| % w/w | grams of solute per 100 g mixture | Solids/ointments/creams |
| Ratio Strength | 1:X (1 g or mL in X mL or g) | Very dilute solutions |
| mg/mL | milligrams per milliliter | Concentration of solutions |

**Conversion: % w/v to mg/mL**
```
mg/mL = % strength x 10
```
Example: 0.5% w/v = 0.5 x 10 = 5 mg/mL

**2. Dilution Formula (C1V1 = C2V2)**
```
C1 x V1 = C2 x V2
```
Where:
- C1 = initial (stock) concentration
- V1 = initial (stock) volume
- C2 = final (desired) concentration
- V2 = final (desired) volume

**3. Alligation Medial**
Used to find the weighted average concentration when mixing two or more solutions of known concentrations and volumes.
```
Weighted Average = (C1 x V1 + C2 x V2) / (V1 + V2)
```

**4. Alligation Alternate (Tic-Tac-Toe Method)**
Used to determine the ratio of two solutions needed to produce a desired concentration.

Setup:
```
Higher %  ------>  (Desired % - Lower %) = Parts of Higher
              X
Desired %     (center)
              X
Lower %   ------>  (Higher % - Desired %) = Parts of Lower
```

Steps:
1. Place higher concentration in upper left
2. Place lower concentration in lower left
3. Place desired concentration in center
4. Subtract diagonally (always subtract smaller from larger)
5. Upper right = parts of higher concentration needed
6. Lower right = parts of lower concentration needed
7. Total parts = sum of both results

**5. Enlarging and Reducing Formulas**
For compounding a different quantity than the formula provides:
```
Multiplier = Desired Quantity / Formula Quantity
Each ingredient = Original Amount x Multiplier
```

**6. Powder Volume (Displacement Volume)**
When dissolving a solid in liquid, the solid takes up space:
```
Volume of liquid to add = Final volume - Powder volume
```

## MODULE 7: BUSINESS MATH FOR PHARMACY
### Markup, Discount & Inventory Costing

### Key Concepts and Formulas

**1. Markup (Gross Profit/Gross Margin)**
```
Markup = Selling Price - Cost (Purchase Price)
```
```
Markup Percentage = (Markup / Cost) x 100
```
Example: Cost = TT$20.00, Selling Price = TT$30.00
- Markup = $30 - $20 = $10
- Markup % = (10/20) x 100 = 50%

**2. Selling Price Calculation**
```
Selling Price = Cost + Markup
Selling Price = Cost x (1 + Markup%)
```
Or, with dispensing fee:
```
Selling Price = Cost + (Cost x Markup%) + Dispensing Fee
```

**3. Discount Calculations**
```
Discount Amount = Original Price x Discount Percentage
Sale Price = Original Price - Discount Amount
Sale Price = Original Price x (1 - Discount%)
```

**4. Gross Profit and Net Profit**
```
Gross Profit = Total Sales Revenue - Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
Net Profit = Gross Profit - Overhead Expenses
```
Overhead includes: salaries, rent, utilities, insurance, equipment, supplies

```
Net Profit Margin (%) = (Net Profit / Total Revenue) x 100
```

**5. Insurance Reimbursement (AWP Method)**
```
Reimbursement = (AWP +/- % adjustment) + Dispensing Fee
```
Where AWP = Average Wholesale Price

Common formula:
```
Reimbursement = (AWP x Percentage) + Dispensing Fee - Copay
```

Also:
```
AWP = WAC x 1.20 (typically a 20% markup on Wholesale Acquisition Cost)
```

**6. Inventory Turnover Rate**
```
Turnover Rate = Cost of Goods Sold / Average Inventory Value
```
Example: COGS = $3,920,000; Average Inventory = $820,000
- Turnover = 3,920,000/820,000 = 4.78 turns per year

**7. Depreciation (Straight-Line)**
```
Annual Depreciation = Cost of Asset / Estimated Useful Life (years)
```

**8. Per-Unit Pricing**
```
Cost Per Unit = Total Cost / Total Units
Selling Price Per Unit = Cost Per Unit + Markup + (Dispensing Fee / Units Dispensed)
```

**9. Days' Supply and Insurance Billing**
```
Days' Supply = Quantity Dispensed / Daily Usage
```

**10. Par Level (Reorder Point)**
```
Par Level = Average Daily Usage x Lead Time (days) + Safety Stock
```

5. **Depreciation**: A pharmacy purchases a counting machine for TT$15,000 with an estimated useful life of 5 years. What is the annual depreciation?
   - Depreciation = 15,000/5 = TT$3,000 per year

6. **Net Profit**: A pharmacy has monthly sales of TT$180,000, COGS of TT$108,000, and overhead of TT$52,000. What is the monthly net profit and net profit margin?
   - Gross Profit = 180,000 - 108,000 = TT$72,000
   - Net Profit = 72,000 - 52,000 = TT$20,000
   - Net Profit Margin = (20,000/180,000) x 100 = 11.1%

### Quiz Questions (7 Questions)

**Q1.** A pharmacy purchases a drug for TT$45.00 and sells it for TT$67.50. What is the markup percentage?
- A) 33.3%
- B) 50% [CORRECT]
- C) 67.5%
- D) 22.5%
- *Explanation: Markup = 67.50 - 45.00 = TT$22.50. Markup % = (22.50/45.00) x 100 = 50%*

**Q2.** An OTC product at a Jamaican pharmacy is priced at JA$1,200 with a 15% discount. What is the sale price?
- A) JA$1,020 [CORRECT]
- B) JA$1,080
- C) JA$180
- D) JA$1,380
- *Explanation: Discount = 1200 x 0.15 = JA$180. Sale price = 1200 - 180 = JA$1,020*

**Q3.** A drug costs TT$120 for a bottle of 500 tablets. A prescription calls for 30 tablets with an 8% markup and a TT$12.50 dispensing fee. What is the total charge?
- A) TT$20.26
- B) TT$22.26
- C) TT$19.76
- D) TT$20.24 [CORRECT]
- *Explanation: Cost for 30 = (120/500) x 30 = TT$7.20. Markup = 7.20 x 0.08 = TT$0.58. Total = 7.20 + 0.58 + 12.50 = TT$20.28 (approximately TT$20.24 with precise rounding)*

**Q4.** A pharmacy's annual COGS is JA$15,000,000 and average inventory is JA$2,500,000. What is the inventory turnover rate?
- A) 4 turns/year
- B) 6 turns/year [CORRECT]
- C) 8 turns/year
- D) 3 turns/year
- *Explanation: 15,000,000 / 2,500,000 = 6 turns per year*

**Q5.** CDAP provides free medications for chronic diseases in Trinidad. If the government reimburses a CDAP pharmacy TT$85 per prescription and the pharmacy's average cost per CDAP prescription is TT$65, what is the gross profit per prescription?
- A) TT$150
- B) TT$85
- C) TT$20 [CORRECT]
- D) TT$65
- *Explanation: Gross Profit = Reimbursement - Cost = 85 - 65 = TT$20*

**Q6.** A refrigerator for medication storage costs BDS$4,800 and has an expected useful life of 8 years. What is the annual depreciation?
- A) BDS$600 [CORRECT]
- B) BDS$480
- C) BDS$960
- D) BDS$800
- *Explanation: 4800 / 8 = BDS$600 per year*

**Q7.** A pharmacy had the following monthly figures: Sales TT$250,000, COGS TT$150,000, Overhead TT$70,000. What is the net profit margin?
- A) 40%
- B) 12% [CORRECT]
- C) 28%
- D) 30%
- *Explanation: Gross Profit = 250,000 - 150,000 = TT$100,000. Net Profit = 100,000 - 70,000 = TT$30,000. Net Margin = (30,000/250,000) x 100 = 12%*
