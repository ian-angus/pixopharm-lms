## 1. LEGISLATION GOVERNING PHARMACY PRACTICE

Trinidad and Tobago's pharmacy practice is governed by a layered framework of legislation. The four principal Acts are:

### 1.1 Pharmacy Board Act, Chapter 29:52 (Act 7 of 1960)

**Amended by:** 172/1961, 136/1976, 42 of 1981, 12 of 1998, Act No. 25 of 2003, Act No. 18 of 2006, and the Pharmacy Board Amendment (No. 2) Regulations, 2023.

**Purpose:** "An Act to establish a Pharmacy Board and to control the distribution of drugs."

**Key Structural Provisions:**

**Council Objectives (Section 7):**
The Council has three statutory objectives:
1. To promote and encourage proper conduct amongst pharmacists
2. To promote good relations between pharmacists and doctors
3. To suppress illegal, dishonourable, improper and objectionable practices amongst pharmacists

**Council Composition:** 10 persons, comprising pharmacists and medical practitioners of not less than a specified period of experience, plus representatives from the Medical Council.

**Disciplinary Powers (Section 20):**
The Council may take disciplinary action against any pharmacist who:
- (i) has been guilty of infamous conduct or negligence in a professional respect
- (ii) has been convicted by any Court in Trinidad and Tobago of any offence punishable on indictment
- (iii) has been convicted by any Court outside Trinidad and Tobago of any offence which, if committed in Trinidad and Tobago, would render such person liable to conviction on indictment
- (iv) has been convicted of any offence under the Act

**Complaints Procedure:** Any person may make a complaint against a registered pharmacist to the Secretary of the Board, who refers it to the Board/Council for investigation.

**Registration Requirements:**
- Bachelor of Science in Pharmacy (B.Sc. Pharm) from an approved institution
- Registration with the Pharmacy Board of Trinidad and Tobago
- Completion of a 6-month pre-registration internship (840 hours of training)
- Must acquire a temporary license before starting the pre-registration period
- Training pharmacy must be established for more than 5 years and have a Responsible Pharmacist on staff
- For foreign-qualified pharmacists or CARICOM member state pharmacists, a temporary license from the Board is also required

**Pharmacy Licensing (Part V):**
- All pharmacies must be licensed by the Pharmacy Board
- Must operate under the supervision of a registered pharmacist
- Distributor companies are NOT licensed to sell medication directly to patients
- Even if a distributor employs pharmacists, those pharmacists are not authorized to dispense drugs without a retail pharmacy licence

### 1.2 Food and Drugs Act, Chapter 30:01 (Act 8 of 1960)

**Purpose:** Regulates the manufacturing, importation, sale, and distribution of food, drugs, cosmetics, and medical devices in Trinidad and Tobago.

**Key Provisions:**
- Establishes the Chemistry, Food and Drugs Division (CFDD) as the regulatory authority
- Requires all drugs sold in Trinidad and Tobago to be registered
- Creates the Drug Inspectorate Division (DID) for enforcement
- Defines **Third Schedule Drugs** -- prescription-only medications
- Classifies **New Drugs** requiring separate registration
- Governs labeling requirements, prohibited sale of adulterated/misbranded drugs
- Sections 5-8 create offences with associated penalties

**Drug Registration:**
- Any person, broker, or business wishing to import, manufacture, or sell food or drugs must have them inspected and approved by the Chemistry, Food and Drugs Division
- The CFDD maintains a computerized database for drug registration
- A public list of drugs registered under this Act is maintained and published (2019 edition published via WHO)

**Critical Regulatory Issue -- The Antibiotics Dichotomy:**
Some antibiotics are registered under BOTH the Food and Drug Act and the Antibiotics Act. The legal interpretation that "there are no antibiotics registered in the Food and Drug Act" (since they should fall under the Antibiotics Act exclusively) creates confusion. Research shows 38.5% of pharmacists believed Food and Drug Act registration permits lawful OTC dispensing of antibiotics.

### 1.3 Antibiotics Act, Chapter 30:02 (Act 18 of 1948)

**Purpose:** Primary legislation specifically governing the control and regulation of antibiotics.

**Key Provisions:**
- All antibiotics are classified as prescription-only under this Act
- Antibiotics may only be sold/dispensed on the authority of a valid prescription from a physician, dentist, or veterinarian
- Despite the legal framework, OTC sale of antibiotics still occurs in practice, particularly in the private sector
- Quinolones and sulfur-based antibiotics are commonly dispensed OTC in violation of the Act
- 64.4% of pharmacists surveyed agreed that all antibiotics should fall under the Antibiotics Act exclusively to resolve the dual-registration confusion

### 1.4 Dangerous Drugs Act, Chapter 11:25 (Act 38 of 1991)

**Amended by:** Act 27 of 1994, Act 44 of 2000 (Proceeds of Crime Act), Act 24 of 2019

**Purpose:** "An Act to provide for the control of narcotic drugs and psychotropic substances and to make provision for the confiscation of the proceeds of drug trafficking and other provisions in connection with drug trafficking and matters connected therewith."

See Section 3 (Controlled Substances) below for full details.

## 2. SCOPE OF PRACTICE

### 2.1 Registered Pharmacists

**Legally authorized activities (derived from the Pharmacy Board Act and Ministry of Health career framework):**
- Medication preparation, compounding, and dispensing of prescriptions
- Drug therapy monitoring for contraindications, interactions, allergies, and appropriateness
- Adverse drug reaction identification and reporting
- Drug quality assurance
- Patient counseling on medications
- Clinical testing: blood pressure, blood glucose, and cholesterol testing with follow-up advice
- Chronic disease management support
- Over-the-counter medication guidance and consultations
- Supervision of pharmacy assistants

**Branches of pharmacy practice recognized in Trinidad and Tobago (15 branches):**
1. Clinical Pharmacy
2. Oncology Pharmacy
3. Paediatric Pharmacy
4. Pharmaceutical Manufacturing
5. Geriatric Pharmacy
6. Pharmacy Law
7. Wholesale Pharmacy
8. Academia
9. Hospital Pharmacy
10. Distribution Pharmacy
11. Community Pharmacy
12. National Security Pharmacy
13. Forensic Pharmacy
14. Retail Pharmacy
15. (Not explicitly named -- likely Regulatory/Government pharmacy)

**Required Professional Attributes:**
- Communication and listening skills
- Problem-solving skills
- Organisational and record-keeping skills
- Computing competency
- Reliability and accountability

### 2.2 Pharmacy Assistants / Pharmacy Technicians

**Legal Basis:** Section 24A of the Pharmacy Board Act (inserted by Act No. 25 of 2003):

> "Nothing in section 24 shall be construed as preventing a pharmacy assistant from assisting in -- (a) the dispensing of prescriptions; and (b) the preparation or compounding of drugs, for retail."

**Key Legal Points:**
- Pharmacy assistants MAY assist in dispensing and compounding
- The word "assisting" is critical -- they work UNDER the supervision of a registered pharmacist
- They cannot independently dispense or compound
- The Pharmacy Board Act does NOT create a separate registration category for pharmacy assistants/technicians (unlike pharmacists)
- Training programs are approved by the Pharmacy Board but there is no formal statutory registration process for technicians

**Training Programs Available:**
- **COSTAATT** -- Certificate in Pharmacy Assistant (prepares students to perform as pharmacy assistants under supervision of registered pharmacists)
- **C. Douglas Institute of Learning** -- Pharmacy Technician GYC
- **Synergy Training Ltd** -- 6-month Dispensary Aid Training (approved by Pharmacy Board of T&T)
- **School of Practical Accounting** -- Pharmacy Skills Training (approved by Pharmacy Board of T&T)

**What is RESTRICTED to Registered Pharmacists Only:**
- Independent dispensing of prescriptions (Section 24)
- Independent compounding of drugs for retail (Section 24)
- Operating a pharmacy without a registered pharmacist in supervision
- Holding a pharmacy licence
- Professional accountability for patient outcomes
- Supervision of pharmacy staff
- Clinical testing and patient counseling (as a professional service)

### 2.3 Shopkeepers (Section 27)

The Pharmacy Board Act provides for a **"Licence to shopkeeper to sell certain drugs"** -- a unique provision allowing non-pharmacist shopkeepers to sell limited categories of drugs (likely OTC/household remedies) under a specific licence from the Pharmacy Board. Section 28 makes it an offence to sell drugs to unlicensed shopkeepers.

## 3. CONTROLLED SUBSTANCES

### 3.1 Dangerous Drugs Act, Chapter 11:25

**Scheduling System:**

| Schedule | Contents |
|----------|----------|
| **First Schedule** | Narcotic drugs (opium, cocaine, cannabis/marijuana, and derivatives) |
| **Second Schedule** | Psychotropic substances (regularly amended -- most recently by The Dangerous Drugs (Amendment of the Second Schedule) Order, 2023) |
| **Fourth Schedule** | Precursor chemicals used in illicit drug manufacturing |
| **Fifth Schedule** | Additional provisions |

**Definitions (Section 2):**
- **"Dangerous drugs"** means a narcotic drug listed in the First Schedule or a thing that contains such a drug, OR a psychotropic substance listed in the Second Schedule or a thing that contains such a substance
- **"Coca"** includes leaves from which cocaine can be extracted
- The Act aligns with UN Conventions (1961 Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs, 1971 Convention on Psychotropic Substances, 1988 Convention Against Illicit Traffic)

**First Schedule Substances (Narcotic Drugs) include:**
- Opium (in any form, except alkaloid Papaverine)
- Coca leaves and cocaine
- Cannabis (marijuana) -- note: partially decriminalized in 2019 for personal possession of small quantities
- Morphine and derivatives
- Heroin
- Other opiates and synthetic narcotics as scheduled

**Licensing Requirements (Section 4):**
- A licence is required to deal in any dangerous drugs
- Licences are issued by the Minister/designated authority
- Section 4 licences specify the types of dangerous drugs and the activities authorized

**Prescription Requirements:**
- Dangerous drugs may only be supplied on a valid prescription
- The 2019 Amendment required licensed persons to receive not only a written order but specifically a "prescription" (tightening the requirement)
- Prescriptions must be from authorized prescribers (physicians, dentists, veterinarians)

**Record-Keeping Requirements:**
- Section 7, 8, 9: Specific record-keeping obligations for persons licensed to deal in dangerous drugs
- **Section 10**: Penalty for neglect or refusal to keep records -- this is a specific offence
- All transactions involving dangerous drugs must be documented
- Records must be available for inspection

**Key Offences and Penalties:**

| Offence | Summary Conviction | Conviction on Indictment |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------------|
| **Simple possession** (e.g., marijuana) | Fine: $25,000 / Imprisonment: 5 years | Fine: $50,000 / Imprisonment: up to 10 years |
| **Trafficking** (1 kg or more) | N/A | Fine: $100,000 or 3x street value (whichever greater) / Imprisonment: 25 years to life |
| **Trafficking near schools** (within 500m) | N/A | Fine: $100,000 or 3x street value / Imprisonment: 35 years to life |
| **Contravention of Sections 7, 8, or 9** (record-keeping) | Specific penalties apply | -- |
| **Possession of devices/apparatus** | Section 13 offence | -- |
| **Unlawful use of premises** | Section 14 offence | -- |

**Cannabis/Marijuana Decriminalization (2019 Amendment):**
- The Miscellaneous Provisions Act 2019 partially decriminalized possession of small quantities of marijuana for personal use
- Possession of 30 grams or less of cannabis: no longer results in a criminal record (decriminalized, not legalized)
- Cultivation of up to 4 plants per household permitted
- Trafficking and commercial sale remain illegal with severe penalties
- This does NOT affect pharmacy regulation of cannabis-based medicines

**Pharmacist-Specific Provisions in Dangerous Drugs Act:**
- Section 6: "Pharmacist not to supply dangerous drugs" -- specific prohibition on pharmacists supplying dangerous drugs except in accordance with the Act
- Section 8: "Delivery of dangerous drugs to Minister" -- obligations regarding surrendering dangerous drugs
- Section 11: "Supply of dangerous drugs by two or more medical practitioners" -- special provisions

**Inspectors (Section 12):**
- Inspectors have powers to enter and examine premises
- May inspect records and registers
- Can seize materials suspected of contravention

### 3.2 Drug Classification System in Trinidad and Tobago

The Ministry of Health's **Regulated Medicines and Substances Portal** classifies drugs into the following categories:

| Category | Description | Where Sold | Prescription Required? |
|----------|-------------|------------|----------------------|
| **Over-the-Counter (OTC)** | For self-medication of self-diagnosed minor ailments | Pharmacies AND OTC-licensed establishments | No |
| **Non-Prescription / Freely Sold** | Sold without prescription under pharmacist supervision | Pharmacies only (under pharmacist supervision) | No, but pharmacist supervision required |
| **Third Schedule Drugs (Prescription Only)** | Listed in Third Schedule of Food and Drugs Regulations | Pharmacies only | Yes -- valid prescription from physician, dentist, or veterinarian |
| **Antibiotics** | Kill bacteria or inhibit growth/reproduction | Pharmacies only | Yes -- prescription required under Antibiotics Act |
| **Controlled Drugs** | Additional controls due to potential risks | Pharmacies only | Yes -- valid prescription, restrictive conditions |
| **Narcotics** | CNS depressants with high potential for misuse/dependence/addiction | Pharmacies only | Yes -- highly controlled under Dangerous Drugs Act |
| **Veterinary Drugs** | For animal use | Pharmacies/veterinary suppliers | Veterinary prescription |
| **Deregistered Drugs** | No longer approved -- considered illegal | Cannot be sold | N/A |

**Governing Legislation for Each Category:**
- OTC and Non-Prescription: Food and Drugs Act, Chapter 30:01
- Third Schedule (Prescription): Food and Drugs Act, Chapter 30:01, Food and Drugs Regulations
- Antibiotics: Antibiotics Act, Chapter 30:02
- Controlled and Narcotics: Dangerous Drugs Act, Chapter 11:25
- All categories: Pharmacy Board Act, Chapter 29:52 (licensing and dispensing)

## 4. PATIENT CONFIDENTIALITY & DATA PROTECTION

### 4.1 Data Protection Act, Chapter 22:04 (Act No. 13 of 2011)

**Assent:** 22nd June 2011
**Partial Entry into Force:** 6th January 2012 (Part I and Sections 7-18, 22, 23, 25(1), 26, 28)
**Note:** Some provisions (particularly Part IV governing private organizations and Part V governing penalties) remained unproclaimed for years but are progressively being brought into force. Section 42(a),(b) entered into force subsequently.

**Regulatory Authority:** Office of the Information Commissioner

**This was the first law in Trinidad and Tobago specifically protecting personal data.**

### 4.2 General Privacy Principles (Section 6)

The Act establishes **12 core principles** that apply to ALL persons/organizations who handle, store, or process personal information:

1. **Organizational Responsibility** -- An organization shall be responsible for the personal information under its control
2. **Purpose Identification** -- The purpose for collection shall be identified before or at the time of collection
3. **Knowledge and Consent** -- Knowledge and consent of the individual are required for collection, use, or disclosure of personal information
4. **Limited Collection** -- Collection shall be legally undertaken and limited to what is necessary
5. **Retention and Use Limits** -- Personal information shall only be retained as long as necessary for the purpose collected; shall not be disclosed for other purposes without prior consent
6. **Data Quality/Accuracy** -- Personal information shall be accurate, complete, and up-to-date as necessary
7. **Security Safeguards** -- Personal information is to be protected by appropriate safeguards having regard to the sensitivity of the information
8. **Sensitive Data Protection** -- Sensitive personal information is protected from processing except where otherwise provided for by written law
9. **Policy Transparency** -- Organizations to make available documents regarding their policies and practices for managing personal information
10. **Access and Challenge Rights** -- Organizations shall disclose all documents relating to the existence, use and disclosure of personal information; individuals can challenge accuracy and completeness
11. **Compliance Engagement** -- The individual has the ability to challenge the organization's compliance and receive timely engagement
12. **International Data Transfers** -- Personal information disclosed outside Trinidad and Tobago shall be regulated; comparable safeguards must exist in the receiving jurisdiction

### 4.3 Definition of Sensitive Personal Information (Section 2)

Sensitive personal information includes data revealing:
- Racial or ethnic origins
- Political affiliations or trade union membership
- Religious beliefs or other beliefs of a similar nature
- **Physical or mental health or condition** (directly relevant to pharmacy)
- Sexual orientation or sexual life
- Criminal or financial record

### 4.4 Healthcare-Specific Provisions

**Section 40 -- Healthcare Exceptions:**
Sensitive personal information (including health data) may be processed WITHOUT individual consent when:
- **(i) It is necessary for the healthcare of the individual**
- (ii) The individual has made the information public
- (iii) It is for research or statistical analysis
- (iv) Required by law enforcement
- (v) Determining access to social services
- (vi) Otherwise authorized by law

**Section 45 -- Medical Information Restrictions:**
"Medical information shall not be disclosed by a public body except -- (a) with the consent of the person to whom such information relates; or (b) by order of the Court."

This is a critical provision for pharmacy practice: medical information held by government/public health facilities (including public hospital pharmacies and health centre pharmacies) requires either patient consent or a court order for disclosure.

### 4.5 Application to Pharmacy Settings

**For Public Pharmacies (RHA facilities, public hospitals):**
- Sections 30-34: Collection of personal information must be authorized by law, for law enforcement, or directly necessary for operations
- Must collect directly from individuals when possible
- Must inform individuals of purpose, legal authority, and contact information
- Must ensure accuracy and completeness of information affecting decisions
- Sections 35-37: Must implement reasonable security arrangements; store/access only in Trinidad and Tobago unless individual consents
- Sections 38-46: Use only for original purposes or consistent uses

**For Private Pharmacies:**
- Part IV (private organizations) -- provisions have been progressively proclaimed
- General privacy principles (Section 6) apply regardless
- Healthcare exception for processing sensitive data applies

**Privacy Impact Assessments (Section 47):**
Required for significant projects -- relevant when pharmacies implement new electronic systems.

**Personal Information Banks (Sections 48-51):**
- Must be documented
- Information sharing requires formal agreements
- Data matching requires Commissioner authorization

### 4.6 Information Commissioner

**Appointment:** By the President for 5-year terms
**Qualifications:** 10+ years legal experience plus expertise in relevant fields
**Powers:**
- Conduct audits and investigations
- Order cessation of non-compliant collection practices
- Issue data matching authorizations
- Publish compliance guidelines
- Appoint inspectors with entry and document seizure authority
- Cannot retain obtained information beyond the necessary period

### 4.7 Penalties

**Section 40(4):** Contraventions of sensitive information restrictions constitute offences.
**Part V** establishes penalties for violations including administrative fines and potential criminal penalties depending on severity and organization type.
**Refusal of Access (Section 53):** Heads of public bodies may refuse disclosure only when it reveals another individual's privacy, discloses confidential information, or reveals evaluative materials.

## 5. ETHICS IN PHARMACY

### 5.1 Statutory Basis for Professional Ethics

The Pharmacy Board Act, Section 7, establishes the Council's objectives which include ethical foundations:
- **(a) To promote and encourage proper conduct amongst pharmacists**
- (b) To promote good relations between pharmacists and doctors
- **(c) To suppress illegal, dishonourable, improper and objectionable practices amongst pharmacists**

### 5.2 Professional Conduct Standards

While Trinidad and Tobago does not have a publicly available standalone "Code of Ethics" document comparable to those in jurisdictions like Australia or the UK, ethical standards are embedded in:

1. **The Pharmacy Board Act itself** -- through disciplinary provisions (Section 20) which define actionable misconduct:
   - Infamous conduct or negligence in a professional respect
   - Criminal convictions (domestic or foreign)
   - Conviction of offences under the Act

2. **The Pharmaceutical Society of Trinidad and Tobago (TPSTT)** -- serves as the professional association and advocates for professional standards, though it is not the regulatory body

3. **UWI School of Pharmacy Curriculum** -- includes specific courses in:
   - Pharmacy Law and Ethics
   - Patient counseling skills
   - Professional practice standards
   - The B.Sc. Pharmacy programme includes applied therapeutics with problem-based learning, complementary/alternative medicine, non-prescription drugs, prescription dispensing, pharmacy law and ethics, drug information and literature evaluation, and patient counselling skills

### 5.3 Core Ethical Principles in Trinidad and Tobago Pharmacy Practice

Based on the statutory framework and professional expectations:

1. **Patient Welfare First** -- The pharmacist's primary obligation is to the patient
2. **Professional Competence** -- Maintaining knowledge and skills through continuing education
3. **Confidentiality** -- Protecting patient information (reinforced by Data Protection Act)
4. **Integrity** -- Honest and transparent dealings with patients, colleagues, and authorities
5. **Accountability** -- Taking responsibility for professional decisions and actions
6. **Supervision** -- Properly supervising pharmacy assistants and ensuring safe practice
7. **Lawful Practice** -- Operating within the scope of all relevant legislation
8. **Non-discrimination** -- Providing equitable pharmaceutical care
9. **Collaboration** -- Working effectively with physicians and other healthcare providers (per Section 7(b) of the Act)
10. **Reporting** -- Duty to report adverse drug reactions, counterfeit medicines, and violations of pharmacy law

### 5.4 Disciplinary Process

1. Complaint submitted to the Secretary of the Board
2. Referral to the Council
3. Council investigation
4. If grounds exist, disciplinary hearing
5. Possible sanctions: suspension, removal from register, conditions on practice
6. **Right of Appeal** (Section 21) from Council decisions
7. **Reinstatement** (Section 22) possible after disciplinary action

## 6. OTC vs PRESCRIPTION DRUG CLASSIFICATION

### 6.1 Classification Framework

Trinidad and Tobago uses a multi-tier drug classification system governed by multiple Acts:

**Tier 1 -- OTC (Over-the-Counter)**
- May be sold directly to consumers without a prescription
- Intended for self-medication of self-diagnosed, minor ailments
- May be sold in pharmacies AND OTC-licensed establishments (general shops with appropriate licence)
- Examples: common analgesics, antacids, cough preparations, basic first aid products
- Governed by: Food and Drugs Act, Pharmacy Board Act (shopkeeper licence provisions)

**Tier 2 -- Non-Prescription / Pharmacist-Supervised ("Freely Sold")**
- May be sold without a prescription BUT only under the supervision of a pharmacist at a pharmacy
- NOT available in general shops
- This is a "behind-the-counter" category -- requires pharmacist professional judgment
- Governed by: Food and Drugs Act, Pharmacy Board Act

**Tier 3 -- Third Schedule Drugs (Prescription Only)**
- Listed in the Third Schedule to the Food and Drugs Regulations
- May ONLY be supplied on the authority of a valid prescription issued by a physician, dentist, or veterinarian
- Dispensed only in pharmacies by or under supervision of registered pharmacists
- Governed by: Food and Drugs Act, Chapter 30:01

**Tier 4 -- Antibiotics (Prescription Only)**
- ALL antibiotics are prescription-only under the Antibiotics Act
- Must have prescription from physician, dentist, or veterinarian
- Despite this, enforcement gaps exist; OTC antibiotic sales still occur
- Governed by: Antibiotics Act, Chapter 30:02

**Tier 5 -- Controlled Drugs**
- Subject to additional controls due to potential for misuse
- Require valid prescription with more restrictive conditions
- Governed by: Dangerous Drugs Act, Chapter 11:25, plus relevant sections of Food and Drugs Act

**Tier 6 -- Narcotics**
- Highest level of control
- High potential for misuse, dependence, and addiction
- Prescription-only with highly restrictive conditions
- Detailed record-keeping requirements
- Governed by: Dangerous Drugs Act, Chapter 11:25

### 6.2 Unique Trinidad and Tobago Issues

**The Antibiotics Dichotomy:**
- Some antibiotics are registered under both the Food and Drug Act AND the Antibiotics Act
- This dual registration creates legal ambiguity about whether certain antibiotics can be sold OTC
- The public sector (RHA pharmacies) prohibits OTC dispensing by policy
- The private sector frequently dispenses antibiotics OTC without prescriptions
- Research confirmed "deficient" enforcement of rules and regulations
- Two divisions oversee regulation: Drug Inspectorate Division (DID) and Chemistry, Food and Drug Division, creating coordination challenges

**Shopkeeper Licence System (Section 27, Pharmacy Board Act):**
- A unique feature of Trinidad's system
- Allows licensed shopkeepers to sell certain limited categories of drugs
- These would be basic household remedies and first aid products
- Must obtain specific licence from the Pharmacy Board
- Section 28 creates an offence for selling drugs to unlicensed shopkeepers

## 7. CARICOM CONTEXT

### 7.1 Trinidad and Tobago's Role in Regional Pharmaceutical Regulation

Trinidad and Tobago holds a **central position** in Caribbean pharmaceutical governance:

**CARPHA Headquarters:**
- The Caribbean Public Health Agency (CARPHA) is headquartered in Port of Spain, Trinidad and Tobago, at 16-18 Jamaica Blvd, Federation Park
- Legally established July 2011 by Inter-Governmental Agreement; operational since January 2013
- This makes Trinidad the physical hub of regional pharmaceutical regulation

**Caribbean Regulatory System (CRS):**
- Located within CARPHA's campus in Port of Spain
- Established as an initiative of 15 CARICOM Member States
- Agreed upon by Ministers of Health in 2014
- Underpinned by the Caribbean Pharmaceutical Policy adopted by Ministers of Health in 2011
- Serves approximately 17 million inhabitants across the region

### 7.2 CRS Functions and Operations

**Reliance-Based Model:**
- All medicines submitted to CRS must have prior approval by a designated reference authority
- Reference authorities include: US FDA, Health Canada, European Medicines Agency (EMA), WHO Prequalification Programme, and 10 Latin American regulatory agencies (PAHO-designated from Argentina, Brazil, Canada, Chile, Colombia, Cuba, Mexico, and the United States)

**What CRS Does:**
- Conducts "abbreviated reviews of product dossiers for safety, quality, and efficacy"
- Verification review (not full independent assessment) -- leverages prior approvals from mature regulatory authorities
- Target time for scientific assessment: within 90 days
- Products must appear on WHO Essential Medicine List or PAHO Strategic Fund

**Key Assessment Elements:**
- Market authorization from reference authorities
- Product formulation and packaging consistency
- Manufacturing site GMP certificates
- Stability data for Zone IVB climatic conditions (tropical)
- Clinical trial or bioequivalence summaries
- Safety reports and pharmacovigilance data

**Products Approved:**
- Anti-retrovirals, antibiotics, treatments for NCDs
- Hepatitis C treatments, cholera vaccines
- Biosimilars (e.g., pegfilgrastim)
- Products previously unavailable across the region

**Funding Model:**
- Initially funded by a Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation grant
- Transitioned to self-sustainability through user fees (November 2019):
  - $300 USD per medicine application (manufacturers)
  - $150 USD per medicine application (local importers)

### 7.3 CRS Member States

**Full Members (15):** Antigua and Barbuda, Bahamas, Barbados, Belize, Dominica, Grenada, Guyana, Haiti, Jamaica, Montserrat, Saint Kitts and Nevis, Saint Lucia, Saint Vincent and the Grenadines, Suriname, Trinidad and Tobago

**Associate Members (5):** Anguilla, Bermuda, British Virgin Islands, Cayman Islands, Turks and Caicos Islands

### 7.4 VigiCarib (Regional Pharmacovigilance)

- Launched by CARPHA in 2017
- Regional online platform for adverse drug reaction (ADR) and substandard/falsified (SF) product reporting
- Allows healthcare professionals, consumers, and patients to report safety events
- Between October 2017 and November 2020: received 233 ADR reports from participating countries
- Connected to WHO Uppsala Monitoring Centre
- ADR reporting rates remain low due to lack of awareness and limited healthcare professional engagement

### 7.5 TECHPHARM

The Technical Advisory Committee on Pharmaceutical Policy (TECHPHARM) provides policy and technical guidance to CARICOM on pharmaceutical matters.

### 7.6 Key Challenges

- Only 5 CARICOM countries currently conduct independent review of safety, quality, and efficacy of drugs
- Larger reference authorities often withhold confidential assessment details
- Member states have varied regulatory capacity; some lack basic infrastructure
- No harmonized Caribbean-wide pharmacy technician certification standard
- CRS provides recommendations but member states retain sovereign authority and must independently review and approve

### 7.7 Trinidad and Tobago's National Regulatory Role

**Farz Khan** (Chief Chemist and Director, Chemistry Food and Drugs, Ministry of Health, T&T) has been a vocal advocate for the CRS, stating the need to "support all areas of medicine regulation, ensuring that local assessment and registration processes are based on international best practices, harmonised requirements and guidelines."

Trinidad's CFDD participates in:
- PAHO/WHO Pan American Network for Drug Regulatory Harmonization (PANDRH)
- Regional pharmacovigilance through VigiCarib
- CRS verification review processes
- Caribbean Pharmaceutical Policy implementation

## 8. HEALTH SYSTEM STRUCTURE

### 8.1 Overview

Trinidad and Tobago operates a **dual public-private healthcare system**:
- **Public sector**: Funded by government through general taxation; services free at point of access
- **Private sector**: Fee-for-service; preferred by those who can afford it for shorter wait times and greater physician attention
- The Ministry of Health is responsible for leading, financing, regulating, and governing the national health sector

### 8.2 Regional Health Authorities (RHAs)

The **Regional Health Authorities Act No. 5 of 1994** decentralized healthcare delivery from the Ministry of Health to five autonomous statutory bodies:

| RHA | Region Covered | Key Facilities |
|-----|---------------|----------------|
| **North-West Regional Health Authority (NWRHA)** | North-west Trinidad including Port of Spain | Port of Spain General Hospital |
| **North Central Regional Health Authority (NCRHA)** | North-central Trinidad | Eric Williams Medical Sciences Complex, Mt. Hope |
| **Eastern Regional Health Authority (ERHA)** | Eastern Trinidad | Sangre Grande Hospital |
| **South-West Regional Health Authority (SWRHA)** | South and south-west Trinidad | San Fernando General Hospital |
| **Tobago Regional Health Authority (TRHA)** | Tobago | Scarborough General Hospital |

**RHA Functions (Section 6 of the Act):**
- (a) Provide efficient systems for the delivery of health care
- (b) Collaborate with UWI and other training institutions in education, training, and research in medicine, nursing, dentistry, **pharmacy**, bio-medical and health science fields, veterinary medicine, and related fields
- (c) Collaborate with and advise municipalities on matters of public health
- (d) Operate, construct, equip, furnish, maintain, manage, secure, and repair all property

**RHA Oversight:**
- Ministry of Health allocates annual budgets to each RHA
- Chairmen distribute and manage funds within their regions
- RHAs submit quarterly spending and performance reports

### 8.3 Public Health Facility Network

- **9 public hospitals** (including 4 specialized: maternity, psychiatric, thoracic, radiotherapy/physical medicine/gerontology)
- **9 regional health facilities**
- **96 health centres** providing primary care
- Approximately **2.7 beds per 1,000 persons** (2015)
- Health centres provide: basic dental care, vaccinations, peri/neo-natal care, outpatient psychiatric care, family planning, dermatological services, urgent care, and general internal medicine

### 8.4 Pharmacy Services in the Health System

**Public Sector Pharmacies:**
- Operate within RHA hospitals and health centres
- Dispense free medications to patients (when available in stock)
- Part of the Ministry of Health's pharmaceutical supply chain
- Central Medical Store (managed by NIPDEC) procures and distributes medications

**Private Sector Pharmacies:**
- Over **280 pharmacies** nationwide (of which 250+ participate in CDAP)
- Approximately **70%+ are private community pharmacies**
- Also includes private hospital pharmacies
- Operate on a commercial fee-for-service basis
- Must be licensed by the Pharmacy Board and supervised by a registered pharmacist

### 8.5 Chronic Disease Assistance Programme (CDAP)

**Launch:** February 2003 by Ministry of Health
**Purpose:** Provide free prescription drugs for chronic disease management
**Administrator:** NIPDEC (National Insurance Property Development Company Limited) in collaboration with Ministry of Health and Pharmacy Board

**Covered Conditions (12):**
1. Diabetes
2. Asthma
3. Cardiac diseases
4. Arthritis
5. Glaucoma
6. Mental depression
7. High blood pressure (hypertension)
8. Benign prostatic hyperplasia (enlarged prostate)
9. Epilepsy
10. Hypercholesterolemia (high cholesterol)
11. Parkinson's disease
12. Thyroid conditions

**Drug Coverage:**
- Initially launched with 20 pharmaceutical items (2003)
- Expanded to 37 items, then 47 drugs, now approximately 53 pharmaceutical items
- Recent additions (October 2025): Losartan Potassium 50mg, Clopidogrel Bisulphate 75mg, Risperidone 2mg, Sertraline 50mg, Rosuvastatin 20mg
- New drugs may be added from time to time

**Eligibility:** ALL citizens of Trinidad and Tobago -- no age restrictions

**How It Works:**
1. Patient presents citizenship proof (National ID or computerised birth certificate for minors) to a doctor
2. Doctor examines patient and fills out 3 copies of the CDAP prescription form (on special CDAP pads)
3. Doctor keeps one copy; gives patient two copies
4. Patient takes both copies plus ID to a participating CDAP pharmacy
5. Pharmacy dispenses medications free of charge

**Impact on Pharmacy Practice:**
- 61% of pharmacists reported decreased original brand sales
- 65% noted increased generic drug orders
- 64% identified need for increased staffing
- Competing demands between CDAP and private prescription counseling
- Computerised system implemented (NIPDEC) to streamline processing

**Participating Pharmacies:** 250+ across 5 geographic regions: Tobago, Central, East, South, and North

### 8.6 Health System Challenges

- Only **1.18 physicians per 1,000 persons** (2007 data)
- Brain drain and emigration significantly deplete the workforce
- 2014 data showed only 35% public trust in public healthcare
- Public system users earn substantially less (mean TT$1,245; over two-thirds unemployed outside home)
- The private sector is preferred by higher-income individuals
- Government has partnered with Cuba, India, and UN Volunteer Programme to supplement staff
- 2016 Ministry of Health budget: TT$4.835 billion (7.28% of national budget)

### 8.7 Health Sector Regulatory Authority

The **Health Sector Regulatory Authority** is charged with regulating both public and private health services, developing and enforcing regulatory standards that healthcare providers must meet.

## 9. UNIQUE ASPECTS OF TRINIDAD AND TOBAGO'S SYSTEM

### 9.1 Notable Features for LMS Course Content

1. **Multi-Act Pharmaceutical Framework** -- Unlike many countries with a single Pharmacy Act, Trinidad uses 4+ overlapping Acts (Pharmacy Board Act, Food and Drugs Act, Antibiotics Act, Dangerous Drugs Act), creating complexity for pharmacy professionals

2. **Antibiotics Dichotomy** -- The dual registration of antibiotics under both the Food and Drug Act and the Antibiotics Act creates a unique regulatory gap exploited for OTC sales

3. **Shopkeeper Drug Licence** -- Section 27 of the Pharmacy Board Act allows non-pharmacist shopkeepers to sell limited drugs, a distinctive feature not common globally

4. **Section 24A Pharmacy Assistant Provision** -- Legal recognition of pharmacy assistants' role in dispensing and compounding, but only in an "assisting" capacity under pharmacist supervision -- no independent registration

5. **CDAP as Public-Private Bridge** -- The programme uses private pharmacies to deliver a public health mission, creating a unique operational model

6. **CARPHA Hub** -- Trinidad hosts the regional regulatory headquarters, giving it a central role in Caribbean pharmaceutical governance

7. **Enforcement Gaps** -- Consistently reported weak enforcement of pharmaceutical regulations, particularly regarding antibiotic OTC sales

8. **Tropical Climate Considerations** -- Zone IVB climatic conditions require specific stability data for drug registration, affecting storage and supply chain management

9. **Cannabis Partial Decriminalization** -- Since 2019, personal possession of 30g or less and home cultivation of up to 4 plants decriminalized, but pharmacy regulation of cannabis medicines remains under the Dangerous Drugs Act

10. **Small Domestic Manufacturing** -- Trinidad imports most pharmaceuticals, making import regulation and supply chain management critical pharmacy competencies
